Abstract
Medical education assessment plays a critical role in safeguarding patient safety, yet current approaches to ensuring question quality are largely reactive and resource-intensive. Poorly constructed distractors in multiple-choice questions (MCQs) may allow examinees with knowledge gaps to pass assessments, potentially compromising clinical decision-making. This study investigates whether distractor-targeted fine-tuning of pre-trained embedding models can support a proactive and scalable framework for pre-deployment quality assurance. Using 6,000 medical MCQs from eight clinical disciplines (16,800 distractor samples with observed selection rates), we compared ten pre-trained embedding models, including five general-purpose and five medical domain-specific ones. Distractor-targeted fine-tuning captured semantic nuances of plausible but incorrect options. Across 49 experimental configurations, we evaluated loss functions, training strategies, learning rates, cross-disciplinary generalizability, and compatibility with parameter-efficient fine-tuning. The proposed approach improved distractor effectiveness prediction, achieving a Pearson correlation of 0.653 (95% CI: 0.626–0.680), representing a 48.6% improvement over baseline (r = 0.439; p < 0.001; Cohen’s d = 1.19). The model demonstrated strong cross-disciplinary generalizability (r ≥ 0.60) and particularly strong performance in the mid-difficulty range (MAE = 0.028). We present a validated transfer learning framework for automated pre-deployment item quality assurance, enabling scalable, real-time screening of MCQs prior to high-stakes medical examinations. 
Introduction
Multiple-choice questions (MCQs) constitute the predominant assessment modality across medical education, spanning undergraduate curricula, board certification examinations, and continuing medical education programs1,2. Their widespread adoption reflects practical advantages including efficient administration, objective scoring mechanisms, and comprehensive content coverage. However, the validity of these assessments depends on distractor quality, that is, the incorrect options that must simultaneously satisfy the competing demands of plausibility and discriminability3,4.
Well-constructed distractors effectively discriminate between examinees with varying knowledge levels and provide diagnostic information about specific misconceptions5. Conversely, ineffective distractors compromise measurement precision and introduce construct-irrelevant variance into assessment scores6. Moreover, the quality of medical education assessment is not merely an educational concern but also an important safeguard for patient safety7. Inadequate question design, particularly poorly constructed distractors, may lead to the certification of physicians with unrecognized knowledge gaps4,8. Creating high-quality distractors remains a persistent challenge for medical educators9. Most evaluation methods rely on analyzing examinees’ response data after the exam, which requires substantial time and effort and provides limited guidance for developing future items10. To address this gap, we introduce an AI-driven framework for quality assurance during the item development stage, before exams are delivered. The framework detects potentially flawed distractors early in the process, allowing educators to refine items prior to deployment. In this way, it provides a proactive safeguard for maintaining the competence of the healthcare workforce.
Transformer-based language models have led to significant advances in medical natural language processing11,12. Text embedding models represent textual content as dense, high-dimensional vectors, enabling the quantification of semantic relationships through mathematical distance13. Given that the efficacy of a distractor is often contingent upon its semantic overlap with the key, these models offer significant potential for the automated evaluation of distractor quality. However, general-purpose embeddings may inadequately capture the specialized semantic relationships inherent in medical MCQs, where effective distractors must strike a delicate balance between semantic proximity to correct answers (ensuring surface-level plausibility) and conceptual distinction (maintaining logical incorrectness).
To overcome these domain-specific limitations, transfer learning through fine-tuning offers a principled approach to domain adaptation14,15. By updating model parameters on domain-specific datasets, this process allows the model to capture specialized linguistic patterns and representations tailored to medical objectives. Fine-tuning has significantly improved performance in various medical natural language processing (NLP) tasks11,16. However, few studies have used fine-tuning to automatically evaluate distractor quality, or to examine how such evaluations might better prepare students for the medical workforce. Standard fine-tuning approaches typically adapt large language models (LLMs) by updating model parameters using task-specific datasets, allowing the model to learn representations tailored to a downstream objective17. While this paradigm has proven effective across many NLP applications, it often relies on modifying large numbers of model parameters and may require substantial computational resources and labeled data. In contrast, embedding-based approaches leverage fixed or lightly adapted vector representations of text18, capturing semantic relationships in a compact and reusable form19. Such representations provide several practical advantages, including computational efficiency20, modular integration with downstream models, and improved scalability in scenarios where full model fine-tuning is impractical. These characteristics make embedding-based approaches particularly attractive for applications such as automated item quality analysis, where semantic relationships among question options play a critical role. Specifically, standard fine-tuning approaches often treat distractors as generic text segments, failing to model their unique role as “plausible but incorrect” alternatives21,22. This lack of functional modeling creates a significant barrier to precisely quantifying distractor effectiveness.
To address this, we propose a distractor-targeted fine-tuning strategy designed to optimize embedding models for assessing distractor effectiveness. By bridging the gap between specific NLP fine-tuning techniques and system-wide examination quality control, this study provides a blueprint for integrating AI into the medical education assessment infrastructure. We address three primary research questions:
Does distractor-targeted fine-tuning substantially improve distractor quality prediction compared to baseline frozen embedding approaches? What training configurations, such as loss functions, training strategies, and learning rates, optimize prediction accuracy? How do architectural factors, including model size, domain-specific pre-training, and cross-disciplinary generalizability, influence fine-tuning effectiveness and deployment scalability?
